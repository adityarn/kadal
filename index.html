Ethical considerations for ocean-based carbon dioxide removal technologies

First published: 5th of April 2024

Climate change emerges as a direct result of the historical and ongoing economic models rooted in capitalism and global trade, fostering and perpetuating global inequality. This period has seen a significant transfer of material and labor from the Global South to the Global North. As the planet warms, largely due to carbon emissions from the economic activities of the Global North or those serving its interests, the capacity to adapt to climate change predominantly resides within the Global North. Meanwhile, large populations face energy poverty and lack access to essential life necessities like food, shelter, and energy.

It is in this context that we see modern attempts at mitigating and removing carbon emissions from our climate system. Multiple new organisations aim to remove carbon by deploying large scale carbon dioxide removal (CDR) technologies that are currently unproven and may result in ecological damage. Much of the work on long term policy and action is taking place in countries that have the money to back up their vision. This is an exclusionary process that does not take into account the perspectives of people from the Global South.

An example from the recent past is the carbon credit system which is a business as usual approach where polluters continue to pollute, but are able to purchase the right to pollute from poorer parts of the world. This incentivizes the lack of development of the Global South economies that seek to participate in these markets, where their economies would primarily be engaged in the task of deploying CDR technologies. It is essentially a market-based approach seeking to address a market failure that in the past treated environment pollution as a “negative externality”.

At a talk given by the CEO of a “non-profit” organisation engaged in ocean CDR research, I was amazed to hear the plan that their organisation envisages for our future planet. It is indeed true that reducing emissions is no longer enough if we would like to stay below 2 to 2.5ºC warming by the end of the century. This requires carbon drawal, or negative emissions. In this future world, a large fraction of additional renewable energy production capacity will be used to run enormous machines that will become part of the CDR network. In such a world a large proportion of material and resources would go towards building this new economy of CDR technologies. This means that currently energy starved populations will have to compete with the CDR sector for access to energy, pushing the price of energy up for everyone. This very model is being marketed as a progressive economic model for the Global South. Their existence in this world is to service the needs of the Global North, where consumption will continue to drive emissions, and the carbon offsets will have to come from a massive deployment of as yet untested technologies with possible dire consequences for the environment in the Global South.

The poor will have to keep cleaning up what the rich choose to dump into the atmosphere.

Organisations engaged in ocean CDR seek to operate in the global ocean. However, for easy access to deployment sites, they need to be situated close to the coast. This would mean that the primary sites of such CDR deployment will not be over remote parts of the deep ocean, but close to coastal communities, close to waters which coastal communities rely on for their food. People in the Global North have been very vocal about their lack of confidence in unproven and untested technologies. In some cases, the German public has even been resistant towards the deployment of renewable sources of power such as wind turbines and solar plants. They have also protested against the deployment of solar geoengineering technologies. This leaves the Global South as the most politically suitable site for deploying untested CDR and mitigation technologies. Weak environmental regulatory capacity within public institutions in these countries allow for a more fuss-free deployment of potentially harmful technologies. Further, countries with more autocratic governments are able to quell civil resistance movements more thoroughly. Elite capture of power in the Global South, with the active help of the Global North has led to a situation where the amount of money that these new economies might bring in will almost always trump the concerns of local populations.

How can we get more participation of voices from the Global South in such initiatives? It is not enough for these organisations to simply say that they are focussing on more global outreach. Any solution to come out of the Global North requires the participation of the Global South. Such participation needs to be on equal terms on a mutually beneficial basis. Much of what has happened in the past has been one sided. For example, with BECCS (bioenergy with carbon capture and storage), most of the social and ecological burdens are borne by the Global South while the richer economies benefit from the carbon credits that they are able to purchase from the large-scale diversion of agricultural land in the Global South.

COVID was an example when there was a global crisis. Much was said about how the whole world was in it together. But when it came to actual action, the Global North cornered much of the vaccine supply with many North American and European countries having secured enough vaccine doses to vaccinate their populations 5 times over. This is an example of how the disproportionate resources that are available to richer countries can quickly be leveraged to gain exclusive access to solutions that will allow their people and societies to go back to living life as normally as possible. This however, leaves out a large chunk of the global population to essentially fend for itself. The Global South is at once a solution and a potential source of unwanted climate refugees for the Global North. The solution being pursued is a way to prevent the worst outcomes of climate change while ensuring that they deploy resources to adapt, build walls, install cooling and so on while continuing to consume at the same level. This is not just unjust to the Global South, but is unjust to the poor within the Global North who also suffer from the lack of access to resources, private and public, to mitigate and adapt to the changing climate.

Historically, the Global North has fiercely protected access to technology through unjust IPR regimes while calling for greater global trade. Today, the Global North has given up on the façade it had put up of supporting greater global trade. By blocking appointments to the WTO commission, the USA has engineered a situation where it is able to circumvent the same rules that it had so fiercely enforced upon developing countries. Today’s erection of trade barriers against China is another example of how the Global North is willing to change its stance on global trade whenever it suits its own immediate geopolitical needs. Further, China is prevented from accessing advanced computer chips technologies which are a key element in the artificial intelligence sector. It is in such a context that technological optimists are functioning. We have no guarantee that future CDR technologies will be accessible to everyone.

This is not to say that technological solutions such as geoengineering or CDR should not be explored at all. The primary focus of this article has been at the moral framing of such activities, which remain blind to the needs of the global poor, both in the South and in the North. Techno-optimists are failing to take these concerns into consideration. Organisations engaged in building ocean CDR policy will benefit from hiring more people from indigenous and under-represented communities in the Global North and more people in general from the Global South. They also need to hire more experts from the social sciences and humanities so that they bring in the diverse perspectives that are currently missing in their overall vision.

The Arctic ice cap is rapidly melting, but what is happening to the Antarctic?

First published: 15th February, 2023

The polar regions are well known for their ice caps. In Antarctica, the land is covered in kilometers thick ice sheets, while the ocean surrounding the landmass freezes to form a large sea ice field extending to about 20 million kilometers every winter. Sea ice forms when the cold polar air freezes the surface seawater during winter. Sea ice is an important component of the Earth’s climate system. Ice is highly reflective, hence sheltering the seawater below from solar radiation. Ice rejects brine as it forms and makes the surrounding waters salty, hence making the polar regions very weakly stratified. This allows for efficient mixing of deep warm waters with cooler surface waters and is an important part of the global thermohaline circulation. Sea ice is a critical component of the planet’s climate and understanding how sea ice will respond to the changing climate is essential if we want to to accurately represent and forecast climate in our computer models.

Both the poles have seen summer sea ice declining over the last few years, with the Arctic having seen a decline for multiple decades. Sea ice extent during summer have fallen at about -8% per decade since the 1950s. The decline of sea ice around Antarctica began more recently, around the year 2016. February 2023 may break all records for the lowest sea ice extent in the Southern Ocean! This decline is mainly driven by the excess heat that we have put into the climate system by emitting greenhouse gases.

Unintuitive though it may be, the warming climate did not result in a reduction of sea ice in the Antarctic until 2016! In fact, sea ice concentration increased by about 12% in the period from 1979 – 2011. During the same period, the surface air has been warming over the Southern Ocean. The response of sea ice to a warming climate is quite complex as it is affected by the radiation in the atmosphere, the winds, the precipitation, the ocean currents, and the melting of glaciers along the coastline. Further, the overall trend of increasing sea ice hides regional differences. The West Antarctic seas saw a decline in sea ice over this period of time. But the increase in sea ice in other regions was larger, thus leading to an overall increasing trend.

The reasons are still a matter of scientific inquiry, but we have good candidate answers that can explain the trends we have seen this far. I will explain the current state of the science around this crucial question – why did the Southern Ocean sea ice field take so long to start melting under a warmer climate?

Before considering candidate answers to our question, we need to understand the factors that affect the sea ice field. Heat can come from above or from below. The atmosphere controls the radiative heat transfer to the ocean, the winds can carry heat away and also control the rate of evaporation, and finally precipitation also results in heat transfer. The winds push sea ice, and depending on the pattern of winds, sea ice can be bunched together or pushed apart. Coastal winds act to push sea ice away, making coastal regions a site of production and export of sea ice.

From below, the ocean circulation affects how heat moves around the global ocean. In the polar regions, surface waters are colder than waters at depth. If the deep water can make it to the surface, it can elevate the temperatures there. This upwelling of deep waters is controlled by the global thermohaline circulation – the large scale overturning of waters from the equator to the poles. Ocean currents and the westerly belt of winds control the upwelling of these deep waters (click here to see a video of deep waters spiralling upwards around Antarctica). Additionally, the surface stratification of the ocean controls the amount of upwelled waters that can mix with the surface waters. The amount of freshwater that flows into the ocean from melting glaciers and from atmospheric precipitation determine how strongly or weakly stratified the surface ocean is. Weaker surface stratification allows more of the warm deep water to mix with the surface, whereas, fresher waters are lighter and they tend to inhibit the mixing of upwelled deep waters.

Now that we understand that sea ice is found within a complex web of interactions between the atmosphere, ocean, and the cryosphere, we can start exploring the factors that led to sea ice growth from the 1970s to 2016, and the eventual decline since then.

One of the leading theories suggests that the ocean played a major role in our story. A study based on computer models was able to replicate the trends that satellites have observed and found that increasing air temperatures first acted to suppress sea ice growth. The amount of sea ice is determined by how much ice grows in a season versus how much is melted away. Increasing air temperatures slowed down the growth of sea ice. Sea ice does not retain much of the salt and tends to reject brine as it grows, thus dumping a lot of salt into the surface water. The suppression of sea ice growth freshened the surface waters. Fresher waters are more strongly stratified, losing their connection with the warmer waters that lie at depth. With lesser heat making it to the surface from the deep ocean, less heat was available to melt the sea ice. So we had a situation where sea ice growth slowed down due to warmer air temperatures, but the melting of sea ice due to warm water supply from deep below slowed down even more, hence leading to an overall buildup of sea ice!

But then, what led to the reduction in sea ice since the year 2016? Once again, our answer seems to lie with the warm deep waters. Deep waters rise to the surface in giant ocean gyres. Liping Zhang and their co-authors used computer simulations of the Earth’s climate to find that there was a buildup of heat in the subsurface ocean, largely due to stronger winds. Winds in the Southern Ocean are responsible for driving a vertical pump (Ekman pump) that pulls warm waters from deep below. They do this by pushing surface waters apart horizontally and creating a drop in height of the surface of the sea. The spinup of the winds drove a stronger vertical pump which led to the buildup of heat below the surface layer of the ocean. Warmer waters are lighter, making the subsurface light enough to eventually break the stratification which allowed greater mixing of warm waters with surface waters. This process may be the reason why sea ice reduced since 2016 in the Southern Ocean.

The question though is not yet settled! There are many changes occuring to the atmosphere, our climate, the cryosphere, the winds and the major gyre systems that we don’t fully understand. These uncertainties are some of the largest uncertainties in climate model simulations that inform the IPCC reports. It is becoming more and more clear to people across the world that our climate is undergoing rapid change, and governments would like more fine-grained forecasts of our future climate. Settling these uncertainties in the polar regions would go a long way in addressing that need!
